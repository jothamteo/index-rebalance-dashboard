Every formula on the dashboard is derived below. Honest about assumptions, explicit about limitations. This page mirrors the source-of-truth methodology document in the tracker repo.

§1 — Event-Study Methodology (Brown-Warner 1985)

For each addition / deletion event with effective date $T_E$ and approximated announcement date $T_A = T_E - 5$ trading days, we estimate a market model on a 250-day pre-event window ending 30 days before $T_A$.

Estimation regression:

$$R_{i,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i R_{m,t} + \varepsilon_{i,t}, \qquad t \in [T_A - 280, T_A - 30]$$

$R_{i,t}$ is the simple daily return of stock $i$; $R_{m,t}$ is the SPY ETF return; the 30-day gap before $T_A$ protects against pre-announcement leakage.

Abnormal return:

$$AR_{i,t} = R_{i,t} - (\hat\alpha_i + \hat\beta_i R_{m,t})$$

Cumulative abnormal return over a window $[t_1, t_2]$:

$$\text{CAR}_i^{[t_1, t_2]} = \sum_{t=t_1}^{t_2} AR_{i,t}$$

The dashboard reports four canonical windows per event: $[T_A-5, T_A-1]$ pre-run-up, $[T_A, T_E-1]$ announcement-to-effective, $[T_E, T_E+5]$ post-effective drift, and the full $[T_A-5, T_E+5]$ window.

§2 — Sector-Matched Control Returns

An alternative AR specification uses GICS sector + size-matched peers. For each event stock $i$ at $T_A$:

  1. Filter the universe to non-event stocks in the same GICS sector.
  2. Rank candidates by $|\ln \text{ADV}_j - \ln \text{ADV}_i|$ where ADV is the 60-day pre-event average dollar volume (size proxy).
  3. Take the closest 5 peers; the control return is their cross-sectional median.

Sector-matched abnormal return:

$$AR_{i,t}^{\text{sector}} = R_{i,t} - \text{median}_{j \in \text{controls}}(R_{j,t})$$

Why ADV not market cap: free data sources don't carry historical-as-of-$T_A$ market cap; ADV is highly correlated with cap within sector and is directly computable from the OHLCV cache.

§3 — Liquidity Estimators

Amihud illiquidity (2002)

$$\Lambda^{\text{Amihud}}_i = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^{N} \frac{|R_{i,t}|}{\text{DollarVolume}_{i,t}}$$

Average daily price impact per dollar of volume over the 60-day pre-announcement window.

Corwin-Schultz spread proxy (2012)

Two-day overlapping pairs, with $H_t$ and $L_t$ the daily high and low:

$$\beta = (\ln H_t / L_t)^2 + (\ln H_{t+1} / L_{t+1})^2$$ $$\gamma = (\ln \max(H_t, H_{t+1}) / \min(L_t, L_{t+1}))^2$$ $$\alpha = \frac{\sqrt{2\beta} - \sqrt{\beta}}{3 - 2\sqrt{2}} - \sqrt{\frac{\gamma}{3 - 2\sqrt{2}}}$$ $$S = \frac{2(e^\alpha - 1)}{1 + e^\alpha}$$

Negative $\alpha$ floored to zero per the published convention.

Kyle's lambda (daily-data variant)

Tick-rule signed volume $Q_t = \text{sign}(R_{i,t}) \cdot V_{i,t}$, then intercept-free OLS:

$$\hat\lambda = \frac{\sum_t Q_t \, \Delta P_t}{\sum_t Q_t^2}$$

This is a daily-data approximation of Kyle's (1985) intraday formulation; we surface it as a relative liquidity ranking, not an absolute price-impact measure.

§4 — TCA / Implementation Shortfall

Estimated demand for one event:

$$\text{demand}_i = \text{passiveAUM} \times \frac{\text{stockADV}_i}{\sum_j \text{stockADV}_j}$$

(Float-weight proxy via ADV-share-of-index. Real index providers compute weights from float-adjusted shares × price; the documented limitation in §6 explains why we don't have those.)

Square-root market impact (Almgren et al. 2005):

$$\text{cost\_bps} = k \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{q / \text{ADV}} \cdot 10^4$$

where $\sigma$ is daily-return std (60-day pre-event), $q$ is the trade dollar amount, and $k = 1.0$ is the institutional-flow average per Almgren et al.

Implementation shortfall comparison:

$$\text{forced bps} = k \sigma \sqrt{q / \text{ADV}} \cdot 10^4$$ $$\text{spread bps} = \frac{k \sigma \sqrt{q / \text{ADV}} \cdot 10^4}{\sqrt{N}}$$ $$\text{savings bps} = \text{forced} - \text{spread}$$

$N$ is the number of trading days the order is split over (default 5, matching the typical SP500 $T_A \to T_E$ gap).

§5 — Index-Effect Decay

For each year cohort, we aggregate the event-level CAR over the canonical $[T_A, T_E-1]$ window. The dashboard reports mean, median, and IQR (p25, p75) per cohort.

Petajisto (2011) reports a $+8.8\%$ premium for SP500 additions in 1990–2005. Greenwood-Sammon (2022) and others have argued the effect has compressed materially as more arbitrage capital chases the trade. Our empirical result on 234 events from 2010–2026 is a near-zero cohort mean with high cohort-to-cohort variance — consistent with full erosion of the historical premium during our sample window.

§6 — Limitations & Caveats

The dashboard's polish must not overstate the rigor of the underlying data. Specifically:

  1. Free data sources have gaps and survivorship bias. yfinance coverage of delisted tickers is imperfect; some historical events are dropped because we cannot reconstruct their pre-event return path.
  2. Daily OHLCV is not tick-level. Spread proxies and Kyle's lambda are estimators, not measurements. True execution cost requires tick-by-tick data outside this project's free-data scope.
  3. Wikipedia's "Selected changes" historical record is curated by humans and may have omissions. We intentionally did not attempt to verify against S&P press releases (often paywalled).
  4. MSCI Singapore data is sparser than SP500. The free-data scraper finds Singapore-mentioning review URLs but cannot extract ticker-level events from MSCI's PDF-based publication format. Production deployment requires a paid MSCI subscription or vendor data feed.
  5. Sector matching uses GICS only. Alternative classifications (ICB, BICS) might yield different control-group results. We use the current Wikipedia GICS sector for historical events; sector reclassifications affect ~5–10% of stocks over 15 years.
  6. Estimated demand for upcoming events uses aggregate passive AUM, ignoring the heterogeneity of tracker-error budgets and rebalancing windows across fund providers (e.g. Vanguard funds front-run more, iShares less).
  7. Most importantly: the index effect's decay over time means historical results are unlikely to predict future event returns. The dashboard is a research tool, not a trading-signal generator.
